Project Overview

The Problem

Marginalized populations are not receiving the prenatal health care they need. These populations include people of color, trans people, poor people, and uninsured people.

Barriers to prenatal care can include inadequate education, financial constraints, wait times, and distance, as well as general discomfort and fear of discrimination or malpractice when seeking medical care.

The Solution

Prenatal care needs to be accessible to all. The client’s objective was to provide aid and education to those who need it via a mobile app. Through this app prenatal care can become more inviting and accessible.

Using previous research conducted by the client it was found that this care would be best provided through Telehealth communication, an AI chatbot companion, health and pregnancy tracking, and educational videos and articles.

“Of women who gave birth in 2016 … 15.0% of women received inadequate prenatal care. Younger women, women with less education … were the least likely to begin care in the first trimester of pregnancy.”


- National Vital Statistics Reports, 2016

The Experience

User Survey

Our team decided to conduct some informal remote surveying of potential users to get a feel of their prenatal care habits and resources, as well as mobile apps that they might have used for their pregnancies. Lydia and I wrote, distributed, and analyzed the survey results.

Based on the results we developed our primary user: Clara.

Clara

"I need a safe space to ask question and stay on top of my prenatal care."
Info

Age: 27

Gender: Female

Status: 12 weeks pregnant

Location: Greensboro, Georgia, U.S.A.

Bio

Clara is a first time mother based in Georgia. She works as a 9-1-1 dispatcher.

She has a supportive partner but does not have close family or friends in the area who have been pregnant recently and can offer advice.

Her OB-GYN office is about an hour away from her home.

Problems
  • Overwhelmed and confused by the information on prenatal care.

  • Unsure if certain aliments are related to pregnancy or something else.

  • Doesn't feel comfortable enough to ask her doctor all of the questions she has.

  • Seeking a community to ask questions during pregnancy.
Goals
  • To stay up to date with prenatal care obligations.

  • To stay healthy during her pregnancy.

  • To give birth to a healthy baby.
Competitor Analysis

I evaluated Flo, What to Expect, and Ovia.

These are all pregnancy tracker applications. Because the features of the app had already been decided, the primary takeaway from these evaluations was how to organize the features (i.e., information architecture).

Information Architecture

The team spent a lot of time trying to figure out how to best organize all of the features in this app. Some team members preferred a more minimal approach with only two or three tabs, and others preferred a less layered approached that required less digging to find certain functions. We eventually settled on a three tabbed setup with other less used functions being present at the top of each main tab page.

The chatbot, Obi, would be available on all windows for ease of access as we wanted Obi to be the app guide and alternative to search bars and infinity lists.

The Interface

Moodboard

I took the helm when it came to the moodboard and color scheme. In the moodboard, I wanted to make sure that we were keeping inclusivity and reality at the forefront.

I didn’t want what we were doing to sugar-coat pregnancy, I wanted it to communicate to the user in an honest way. To do this in the moodboard I included images of ultrasounds procedures, a rendered fetus, and the stages of the fetus.

But I also wanted it to be airy and soft, as to not complicate the user’s pregnancy experience any further.

Health • Clarity • Empowerment • Guidance

OBI THE CHATBOT

We spent some time discussing how the chatbot should be presented in the app.

We chose a stork because of its cultural relationship to pregnancy and birth. We wanted the chatbot to communicate a sincere friendliness that comes across as thoughtful without being patronizing.

I went ahead and started sketching and thus Obi was born. Using the color palette I developed from the moodbaord I created a digital Obi in Illustrator.

OBI

The name Obi was chosen because it sounds like OB/GYN so it has the credibility of a doctor; Obi also invokes a fun playfulness without it being too “cutesy” so it’s lighthearted and easy to talk to while also being serious and credible enough to talk to about sensitive and scary issues.
